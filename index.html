The Challenge

The Problem Space

The world's most ambitious technological breakthroughs emerge from brilliant technical founders: PhDs, professors, and researchers who've spent decades at the frontier of scientific discovery.

Yet as economists from Schumpeter to Teece have documented, scientific excellence and commercial viability require fundamentally different capabilities. The "valley of death" between laboratory breakthrough and market impact is still very real. They don't know their market, can't articulate value propositions to investors, and struggle to identify the right partnerships to scale.

My Role

At CDL, I built a systematic approach to de-risk these moonshots:

  • Reviewed 200+ technical applications across 5 deep tech verticals
  • Conducted hundreds of founder interviews to assess technical novelty and market potential
  • Worked closely with 50-80 startups over 6 years through 9-month intensive programs
  • Launched CDL-Estonia focused on cybersecurity technologies

Space

Autonomous Medical Assistant for Extreme Environments

An ER physician-founder was building the world's first autonomous medical assistant for space applications—starting with a resuscitation app that had won a Canadian Space Agency contract but faced a credibility gap: investors couldn't see the path from a hospital app to a space-ready platform for the ISS. I embedded with the founding team as a strategic thought partner, recruiting space medicine experts to validate the technical roadmap and conducting market analysis across adjacent extreme environment sectors (military, maritime, remote operations). Working hands-on with the founder, I shaped a phased investment narrative that articulated clear value inflection points—from resuscitation-focused MVP to comprehensive autonomous medical assistant—translating complex technical requirements into a business roadmap that secured pre-seed funding and positioned the company for Series A readiness.

Pre-seed secured | Validated roadmap: hospital → space → extreme environments
